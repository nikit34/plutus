How to price digital products (without guessing)

Most creators under-price by 30–50%. A working framework for setting prices, testing anchors, and knowing exactly when to raise. Based on conversion-rate math, not vibes.

Mental model

Price the outcome, not the asset

Creators tend to price based on effort ("I spent 40 hours on this") or size ("60 pages feels like $29"). Buyers price based on outcome: what saving or earning does this unlock?

A 15-page PDF that helps someone land a $75k remote job is worth $99 easily. A 300-page PDF that rehashes general career advice is worth $9. Outcome > effort. Outcome > page count.

First pricing question: what specific, concrete result does the buyer walk away with? Anchor your price against 1% of that result's dollar value.

The three-tier anchor

Why you should always offer three tiers

A standalone $99 product looks expensive. A $99 product sitting between a $29 starter and a $299 premium looks like the obvious middle choice — and it sells 2–3x better than the same $99 product alone.

This is the price-anchoring effect, and it is the single highest-leverage pricing trick available. Create three tiers even if the bottom tier barely sells:

Tier 1 ($9–$29): cheat sheet, reference card, excerpt. Low commitment. Filters out tire-kickers. Builds trust.

Tier 2 ($49–$149): the main product — full guide, full template, full course. Most revenue flows here.

Tier 3 ($299–$799): bundle + personal touch — consultation, community access, extended license. Anchors tier 2. A few high-intent buyers take this.

The data-driven path

Revenue-per-view math: the metric that matters

Conversion rate is a vanity metric. Revenue-per-view (RPV) is the one you actually optimize. RPV = total revenue ÷ total views of the product page.

If 100 visitors buy your $29 product at 3% conversion, that is $87 revenue and $0.87 RPV. If 100 visitors buy your $49 product at 2% conversion, that is $98 revenue and $0.98 RPV — despite lower conversion. The higher-priced product actually performs better per unit of traffic.

Raise prices when RPV is trending up with each increase. Stop raising when RPV flatlines or drops. This replaces the guessing game ("is $X too much?") with a number that tells you.

Benchmarks

Price ranges by product type (2026)

Cheat sheets / checklists: $9–$19. High volume, low barrier.

PDF guides (20–40 pages): $19–$49. The default for expert content.

Notion / Figma templates: $19–$79. Depends on complexity and niche.

Mini-courses (3–5 lessons): $49–$149. Video + workbook bundle.

Full courses (10+ lessons): $149–$499. Anchor against comparable Udemy/Coursera but with higher production value.

Consulting calls: $99–$499 per session. Niche and specificity drive price.

Agency-level packages: $499–$2,499. Bundle of deliverables + support.

These are floors and ceilings, not targets. Niche matters more than format: a $499 PDF for B2B SaaS execs is realistic; a $499 PDF for weekend hobbyists is not.

Practical process

How to set your first price

  1. Find three comparable products. Look for digital products in the same niche. What do they charge? Do not match — use their prices as the range, and position yours within it.
  2. Start at the middle. If comparables are $19, $49, $99 — start at $49. Not the cheapest (signals low value), not the most expensive (no social proof yet to justify).
  3. Watch RPV for 2–4 weeks. Track visitors, sales, revenue. Compute RPV. If RPV is consistent and conversion is >3%, your price is probably under-set. Test a 20–40% increase.
  4. Iterate on three tiers within 2 months. Add a cheaper excerpt product. Add a premium bundle. Rerun the RPV analysis for each tier. Revenue often doubles just from the ladder.
Common mistakes

What creators get wrong

Round numbers. $49 converts slightly better than $50. $99 better than $100. Not enormously, but consistently. Use charm pricing where possible.

Discounting too early. A 30% launch discount sets the baseline; afterwards buyers wait for discounts. Hold price for the first 60 days, then test targeted discounts (newsletter exclusive, Black Friday).

Racing to the bottom. "I'll undercut the competition" is a strategy only for commodity markets. Digital creator products are not commodities — they are bets on your specific expertise.

Stopping at one price. Not testing price is the single biggest money left on the table. Plutus' price-elasticity tips flag exactly when to raise.

FAQ

Good questions

Should I price lower to build reviews/social proof?
For the first week of your first product, maybe — 30% off to early buyers in exchange for a testimonial works. After that, a low price becomes a perception ceiling that is hard to raise.
How often should I raise prices?
Every 3–6 months if RPV is stable or rising at each step. If you are running a ladder (three tiers), rebalance whenever the middle tier is selling too well — that signals it's now perceived as the cheap option.
What about regional pricing / purchasing-power parity?
For most digital products with global audiences, a single USD price works fine. If your niche is heavily concentrated in emerging markets, consider a regional discount code for those countries.
Should I offer a refund policy?
A 30-day no-questions refund policy typically doesn't hurt conversion and protects trust. Real refund rates on digital products usually stay under 3%.
What if my conversion drops after a price increase?
Check RPV, not conversion. If conversion drops 30% but RPV rises 20%, you made money from the increase. Only revert if RPV drops with the new price.
